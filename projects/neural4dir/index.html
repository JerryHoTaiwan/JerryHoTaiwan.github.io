Efficient 4D Infrared Spectroscopy via Neural Field Reconstruction and Adaptive Sampling

Chi-Jui (Jerry) Ho, Harsh Bhakta, Xiong Wei, Nicholas Antipa
Dept. of Electrical and Computer Engineering\, UC San Diego
Dept. of Chemistry and Biochemistry\, UC San Diego
Paper (coming soon) arXiv (coming soon) Code (coming soon)

Neural fields and active sampling enable dense 4D spectra reconstruction from sparse multidimensional IR measurements.

Abstract

Multidimensional infrared spectroscopy offers a window into ultrafast molecular dynamics through rich vibrational couplings and dynamical patterns. However, acquiring these high-dimensional spectra is time-consuming: when collecting 4D spectra—spatially and temporally resolved 2DIR—sampling densely across all dimensions quickly becomes prohibitively slow or infeasible in practice.

To address this challenge, we introduce a neural representation framework to efficiently reconstruct dense 4D spectra from sparse experimental measurements. We model the continuous spectral field with a coordinate-based multilayer perceptron that maps 4D coordinates to corresponding spectral intensities, enabling the recovery of densely sampled spectra from limited observations.

Beyond passive reconstruction, we develop an active sampling strategy guided by reconstruction loss, which adaptively selects the most informative sampling locations. Experimental results demonstrate that our method faithfully recovers oscillatory and dynamical spectral features using only a fraction of the original samples. By integrating neural fields with adaptive sampling, this work points toward practical multidimensional measurements in ultrafast spectroscopy.

Overview

4D infrared spectroscopy as a dense continuous field, reconstructed from sparse 4D samples.

From gridded spectra to continuous fields

Conventional 2DIR and 4DIR experiments acquire spectra on a fixed grid in coherence times and frequencies. Achieving high resolution along spatial $(y, x)$ and temporal/frequency $(t_2, \omega_1, \omega_3)$ axes leads to an explosion in the number of required measurements.

In Neural4DIR, we replace the discrete 4D grid with a continuous spectral field parameterized by a neural network. Given sparse measurements in the 4D domain, the network learns a mapping \[ (y, x, t_2, \omega_1, \omega_3) \mapsto I(y, x, t_2, \omega_1, \omega_3), \] enabling dense interpolation and extrapolation without scanning every location.

This representation allows us to decouple reconstruction fidelity from the raw sampling rate, and to query the field at arbitrary resolutions for downstream analysis such as peak tracking, lineshape evolution, and spatially resolved dynamics.

Neural4DIR overview. Sparse 4D measurements sampled along spatial and spectroscopic axes are fit with a coordinate-based MLP that represents the full 4D field. The learned neural field can be evaluated at arbitrary coordinates to generate densely sampled spectra and derived visualizations.

Method: Neural field reconstruction

Coordinate-based MLPs for oscillatory, multi-peak 4D spectra.

Neural representation of 4D spectra

We represent the 4D spectrum as a neural field: a multilayer perceptron (MLP) that takes normalized coordinates in the 4D space and outputs complex-valued or intensity spectra. Fourier features and peak-aware normalization can be incorporated to capture the highly oscillatory structure and the strong amplitude imbalance between positive and negative peaks.

The model is trained by minimizing a reconstruction loss over the sparse set of observed coordinates, with additional regularization terms that encourage smooth evolution along the waiting time and spatial axes.

Neural field architecture. Input coordinates are embedded with positional encodings or Fourier features and passed through an MLP to predict spectral amplitudes. The reconstruction loss is evaluated only on measured coordinates, while the continuous field can be queried densely at test time.

Results: 4D reconstruction from sparse measurements

Neural4DIR reconstructs oscillatory peaks and dynamical features using a fraction of the original samples.

Reconstruction quality on 4D spectra. Slices of the 4D spectrum are shown as 2D frequency–frequency maps at different waiting times and spatial positions. Neural4DIR preserves diagonal and cross peaks, as well as their temporal beating patterns, even when trained from aggressively subsampled measurements, outperforming conventional interpolation and compressed sensing baselines.

Temporal projections along the waiting time dimension show that Neural4DIR recovers oscillatory dynamics with the correct phase and decay envelopes.

Spatial averages and profiles confirm that the model preserves spatially varying spectral content, enabling spatially resolved 4DIR analysis from sparse data.

Adaptive sampling strategy

Reconstruction-driven active sampling to prioritize informative 4D coordinates.

Loss-guided sampling

Instead of committing to a fixed sampling pattern, Neural4DIR alternates between reconstruction and sampling. After fitting the current neural field, we evaluate reconstruction errors or acquisition utilities over candidate coordinates and select the next batch of measurements at locations that maximize the expected improvement in reconstruction quality.

This active paradigm naturally concentrates measurements near spectral peaks, along sharp features, or in regions where temporal dynamics are most ambiguous, while avoiding redundant sampling in smooth or low-energy regions.

Adaptive sampling. Sampling density progressively concentrates around spectrally and temporally informative regions, reducing the number of required measurements for a target reconstruction quality compared to uniform or heuristic sampling schedules.
